# Mixed Business + Technology Optionality Valuation

Use this method for companies that do **not** fit cleanly into either:

- `PB` heavy-asset cyclicals, or
- `PEG/PSG` pure asset-light growth stocks.

Typical examples:

- Display/material/process companies incubating a semiconductor platform
- Packaging substrate/process companies with legacy revenue plus a high-expectation new technology
- Hybrid names where the market is paying for a future platform, not current earnings

This is the right frame for names like:

- 沃格光电
- Similar "legacy business + TGV / glass substrate / CPO / new packaging platform" stories

## Core Idea

Do **not** force one multiple onto the whole company.

These names are usually:

`Base Business Value + Growth Segment Value + Technology Option Value`

That is a `SOTP + PSG + option premium` framework.

## When To Use This Framework

### ✅ Good fit

- The company has real legacy revenue, but the stock is re-rated by a new technology story
- The new segment is too small for normal PE analysis
- The company is loss-making or barely profitable
- Current market cap clearly cannot be explained by current earnings alone

### ❌ Bad fit

- Pure fabless growth stock with clean segment economics
- Heavy-asset cyclical manufacturer
- Mature profitable company with stable segment mix

## Step 1: Split The Company Into Three Buckets

### Bucket A: Base Business

The existing business that would still exist even if the new technology thesis vanished.

Examples:

- Display backlight / panel-related business
- Mature material processing revenue
- Legacy module or component revenue

How to value it:

- Use low-to-mid `PE` if profitable and stable
- Use low `PS` if margins are weak or earnings are noisy
- Use replacement-value / conservative multiple logic if the business is cyclical or low quality

Question:

`What is this company worth if the new technology story disappears tomorrow?`

That is your floor anchor.

### Bucket B: Visible Growth Segment

The segment that is already shipping, sampling, or entering small-batch supply.

Examples:

- MiniLED ramp
- Early CPO modules
- Initial advanced packaging substrate shipments

How to value it:

- Use `PEG` if already profitable with margin expansion
- Use `PSG` if still loss-making but revenue is growing fast
- If segment data is partial, infer with conservative revenue bands

Question:

`What part of the growth story is already visible in revenue, not just in slides?`

### Bucket C: Technology Option Value

This is the part the market is really speculating on.

Examples:

- TGV / glass substrate becoming a true AI/HPC packaging platform
- CPO platform becoming a strategic node in optical interconnects
- A domestic substitute moving from validation into scaled adoption

This bucket is not normal earnings.

It is an option on:

- qualification
- customer validation
- yield improvement
- volume ramp
- industry timing

Question:

`How much of today's market cap is paying for future strategic positioning rather than current business evidence?`

## Step 2: Value Buckets A and B, Then Back Out Bucket C

Formula:

```text
Technology Option Value
  = Current Market Cap
    - Base Business Value
    - Visible Growth Segment Value
```

This residual is the cleanest way to see what the market is paying for the technology dream.

## Step 3: Judge Whether The Residual Is Rational

Do not ask: `Is the total market cap expensive?`

Ask instead:

`Is the residual option value too large relative to the maturity of the technology?`

Use this stage ladder:

### Stage 0: Concept

- no customer validation
- no process proof
- no shipment

Option value should be small.

### Stage 1: Technical proof

- pilot line
- internal proof-of-concept
- process capability shown

Option value can expand, but still mostly story-driven.

### Stage 2: Customer sampling / joint development

- send samples
- public partner validation
- design-in or co-development language

This is usually where multiple expansion starts.

### Stage 3: Small-batch supply

- real product revenue
- small-batch or initial delivery
- customer acceptance improving

At this stage, the option is no longer pure fiction. But revenue may still be too small to justify a very large residual.

### Stage 4: Volume ramp

- meaningful revenue contribution
- repeat orders
- yield and capacity become the key debates

At this stage, the option should begin converting into visible segment value.

## Step 4: Use Scenario Bands, Not A Single Number

For Bucket C, do not pretend precision.

Use 3 cases:

### Bear case

- technology stalls
- qualification delayed
- low or no scale conversion

Option value compresses sharply.

### Base case

- small-batch progresses into real niche revenue
- market narrative survives
- but large AI/HPC adoption takes longer than bulls expect

### Bull case

- platform becomes a strategic domestic substitute
- customer adoption accelerates
- segment revenue compounds rapidly

The point is not to predict exactly. The point is to understand what the market is already discounting.

## Step 5: Convert The Framework Into A Practical Decision

### If most of market cap = Buckets A + B

The stock is grounded in observable business.

### If a moderate share of market cap = Bucket C

The stock has meaningful but manageable optionality.

### If a very large share of market cap = Bucket C

The stock is mostly a technology option.
You are trading expectation, not business proof.

That means:

- upside can be large
- downside can be violent
- timing matters more than intrinsic value precision

## Suggested Heuristics

These are judgment tools, not hard rules.

### Green

- Base business has real floor value
- New segment already has small-batch supply or clear validation
- Residual option value is not absurd relative to comparable platform stories

### Yellow

- Base business quality is mediocre
- New segment exists but revenue is still tiny
- Most of the upside depends on 1-2 years of execution going right

### Red

- Legacy business weak
- New technology revenue trivial
- Market cap already implies large-scale success

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Using full-company PSG** when only a tiny segment is the real growth engine.
2. **Treating pilot-line success as volume-ramp success.**
3. **Valuing technology option value like normal earnings.**
4. **Ignoring the base business floor** and therefore misunderstanding downside.
5. **Ignoring timing risk** — the technology may work, but not on the timeline the stock price implies.

## Short Version

For hybrid semiconductor-adjacent growth stories:

```text
Equity Value
  = Legacy/Base Business
  + Visible Growth Segment
  + Technology Option Value
```

If the third term dominates, you are not buying current fundamentals.
You are buying a call option on successful industrialization.
